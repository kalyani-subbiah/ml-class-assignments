I performed K-Means cluster analysis on alcohol and volatile acidity using 3 clusters, which were the most important variables from the correlation analysis. When comparing the clustering to the actual plot of variables with wine quality as color, I found that the clustering was roughly related to the wine quality, when wine quality is recoded in three categories.

When volatile acidity is very high, the wine is of low quality, but a high alcohol content and a low volatile acidity produces the best wine. When volatile acidity is high and the alcohol content is low, the wine is of mediocre quality.

def GridSearch_Cluster(method = "KMeans", df = "X", min_clusters = 2, max_clusters = 10, num_columns = len(X.columns)):

"""
Grid-Search for three clustering methods: KMeans, --- and -- using silhouette score

Args:
    method (function): KMeans, --- or ---.
    df (dataframe): dataframe of predictors
    min_clusters (int): minimum number of clusters to search over
    max_clusters: maximum number of clusters to search over
    num_columns: number of columns to search over

Returns:
    Message with maximum score and number of clusters, combination of
    predictors that generate maximum score.
"""

from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score import itertools

total_columns = len(X.columns)

dictionary to store silhouette scores, number of clusters and variables used, keyed by silohouette score¶

silhouette_scores = {}

list of scores to calculate max(score) and key dictionary for values¶

scores = []

comb_count = 0

count = 2

for r in range(2,num_columns):

print(" %i % done ---------------------: ", count)
count += 1
for comb in itertools.combinations(total_columns, r):
    comb_count += 1
    print("combinations completed", comb_count)
    X1 = X[list(comb)]   
    for number in range(min_clusters, max_clusters):
        cluster = method(n_clusters=number)
        cluster.fit(X1)
        y_cluster = cluster.predict(X1)
        score = silhouette_score(X1,y_cluster)
        scores.append(score)
        silhouette_scores[score] = [number,comb]

return("Max score: ", max(scores), "\nOptimum number of clusters and best predictor combination: ", silhouette_scores[max(scores)])

2. Use Hierarchical Cluster Analysis to identify cluster(s) of observations that have high and low values of the wine quality. (Assume all variables are continuous.) Use complete linkage and the same number of groups that you found to be the most meaningful in question 1. Describe variables that cluster with higher values of wine quality. Describe variables that cluster with lower values of wine quality.¶

If you want to make a good bottle of wine, then what characteristics are most important according to this analysis? Have your conclusions changed using Hierarchical clustering rather than k means clustering? Present any figures that assist you in your analysis.¶

I initially used 3 clusters, which I found most meaningful in question 1 for the k-means clustering. I decided to use a dendrogram to analyse the optimal number of clusters, and decided to stick to three clusters, which the dendrogram shows is also a good number of clusters.

The agglomerative clustering analysis in 2D does not reveal any visual clusters when using the entire dataset. However, if the clustering is restricted to two or three predictors, patterns are visible. For example, clustering residual sugar against density and comparing it with the category plot shows that wines with high residual sugar and high density are of high quality. Wines with low residual sugar are of poor quality, regardless of their density.

On the other hand, wines with high residual sugar and low chlorides have better quality, while wines with low residual sugar and high chlorides are of moderate quality. Wines that have low residual sugar and moderate-low chlorides have the worst quality.

Hence, the predictors that were the best for clustering in the case of k-means also form the clearest two-dimensional clusters in case of agglomerative clustering - residual sugars, chlorides, and density. Hence a high amount of residual sugars, low chlorides and density make for a great bottle of wine.

3. Use Principal Components Analysis to reduce the dimensions of your data. How much of the variation in your data is explained by the first two principal components. How might you use the first two components to do supervised learning on some other variable tied to wine (e.g. - wine price)?¶

About 90% of the variance is explained by six factors.

The first two principal components explain about 45.7% of the total variance as indicated by the cumulative explained variance. The PCA components can be used for supervised learning on any other variable like price or quality, since they constitute a large proportion of the variance in the predictor variables and are uncorrelated with each other. Since the components are uncorrelated with each other they are suitable for models that require uncorrelated predictors like linear regression and other models that gain predictive power when using independent predictors like decision trees and SVMs. The components can be used as predictors in these models for better accuracy, though this may lead to reduced understanding of how the model works.
